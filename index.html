Why this calculator?

Most sources that provide dark sky data give raw SQM readings from light pollution maps, without accounting for local conditions. This leads to large discrepancies between the mapped estimate and what you actually experience on site.

This tool corrects for atmospheric conditions — transparency, airglow, aerosols, lunar interference, altitude, and bright planets — to give you a realistic predicted sky brightness.

How to use this tool

Enter the light pollution map’s SQM estimate in Light Pollution Map SQM, then fill in your site’s conditions: altitude, transparency, airglow, and any bright planets above the horizon.

Use Astrospheric.com or Meteoblue to forecast transparency. Results update automatically as you adjust each parameter.

 Source: Light Pollution Map Analysis by David J. Lorenz — methodology basis for this calculator

The Bortle Scale

21.9–22.2NELM 7.6–8.0 — Theoretically darkest sky limited by airglow and starlight

21.7–21.9NELM 7.1–7.5 — Gegenschein visible. Zodiacal light annoyingly bright. Rising milkyway confuses some into thinking it's dawn. Users of large dobsonian telescopes are very happy. Faint shadows cast by milkyway visible on white objects. Clouds are black holes in the sky. No light domes. The milky way has faint extentions making it 50 degrees thick.
3
21.4–21.6NELM 6.6–7.0 — The sky is crowded with stars, extending to the horizon in all directions. In the absence of haze the M.W. can be seen to the horizon. Clouds appear as black silhouettes against the sky. Stars look large and close. [-Richard Berry] Low light domes (10 to 15 degrees) on horizon. M33 easy with averted vision. M15 is naked eye. Milky way shows bulge into Ophiuchus.
4
20.4–21.3NELM 6.1–6.5 — Milkyway shows much dark lane structure with beginnings of faint bulge into Ophiuchus. M33 difficult even when above 50 degrees. The M.W. is brilliant overhead but cannot be seen near the horizon. Clouds have a greyish glow at the zenith and appear bright in the direction of one or more prominent city glows. [-Richard Berry] Some dark lanes in milkyway but no bulge into Ophiuchus. Washed out milkyway visible near horizon. Zodiacal light very rare. Light domes up to 45 degrees.
5
19.4–20.3NELM 5.6–6.0 — To a city dweller the M.W. is magnificent, but contrast is markedly reduced, and delicate detail is lost. Limiting magnitude is noticeably reduced. Clouds are bright against the zenith sky. Stars no longer appear large and near. [-Richard Berry] Milkyway washed out at zenith and invisible at horizon. Many light domes. Clouds are brighter than sky. M31 easily visible.
6
18.6–19.3NELM 5.0–5.5 — M.W. is marginally visible, and only near the zenith. Sky is bright and discoloured near the horizon in the direction of cities. The sky looks dull grey. [-Richard Berry] Milkyway at best very faint at zenith. M31 difficult and indestinct. Sky is grey up to 35 degrees.
7
18.1–18.5NELM 4.0–5.0 — The Milky Way is generally invisible. Skyglow extends across most of the sky and significantly reduces stellar contrast. Even at zenith, the sky looks gray or yellow in bad transparency.
8
17.9–18.0NELM 3.0–4.0 — Entire sky is grayish or brighter. Familliar constellations are missing stars. Fainter constellations are absent. Less than 20 stars visible over 30 degrees elevation in brigher areas. Limiting magntude from 3 to 4.CCD imaging is still possible. But telescopic visual observation is usually limited to the moon, planets, double stars and variable stars.
9
<17.9NELM <3 — Stars are weak and washed out, and reduced to a few hundred. The sky is bright and discoloured everywhere. [-Richard Berry] Most people don't look up.
