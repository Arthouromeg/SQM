This calculator is used to accurately estimate a location's actual darkness. Many sources that are usually used to find dark skies give out raw SQM data, not accounting for the conditions. This can lead to huge differences between the estimated light pollution and what it actually is like over there. This is why I have coded this tool, that can calculate the darkness of an area using atmospheric and other conditions such as transparency, airglow, aerosols, the moon, planets, etc...

How to use this?

To calculate the actual darkness of locations, this calculator comes in handy. Simply insert the light pollution map's estimate in "Light Pollution Map SQM", and then, enter the details for your location, such as altitude, transparency, airglow, etc... You can use websites such as https://www.astrospheric.com to predict transparency and other conditions. The calculation will automatically be done when you change parameters.

The Bortle Scale

BortleSQMWhat does it look like?
121.9–22.2NELM 7.6–8.0 — An untouched night sky with no light pollution at all. The Milky Way is extremely bright and detailed, casting visible structure and even faint shadows on the ground. Zodiacal light, airglow, and deep-sky objects are all easily visible to the naked eye.
221.7–21.9NELM 7.1–7.5 — Very dark skies with only the faintest traces of artificial light. The Milky Way is highly structured and impressive, and faint galaxies like M33 can be seen without optical aid. The sky still feels natural and deeply immersive.
321.4–21.6NELM 6.6–7.0 — A mostly dark rural sky with some light domes near the horizon. The Milky Way remains strong overhead but begins to lose detail closer to the horizon. Dark nebulae are still visible, though less striking than in darker classes.
420.4–21.3NELM 6.1–6.5 — A mix between rural darkness and suburban glow. Light pollution is noticeable in multiple directions. The Milky Way is still visible but clearly weakened, especially near the horizon, and deep-sky detail begins to fade.
519.4–20.3NELM 5.6–6.0 — Moderate light pollution dominates the sky. The Milky Way is faint and often only visible overhead on good nights. Many deep-sky objects require optical aid, and the sky has a noticeable gray or orange glow.
618.6–19.3NELM 5.0–5.5 — Strong light pollution washes out most faint structures in the sky. The Milky Way is very difficult to see or completely invisible. Only brighter deep-sky objects like M31 are detectable, and the sky has a strong artificial glow.
718.1–18.5NELM 4.0–5.0 — Heavy light pollution severely reduces sky visibility. The Milky Way is gone, and only the brightest stars remain visible. Constellations are incomplete, and the sky background is bright and grayish.
817.9–18.0NELM 3.0–4.0 — Severe light pollution dominates the night sky. Only a handful of stars are visible, and even bright deep-sky objects are very difficult to observe. The sky is typically orange or white depending on lighting types.
9<17.9NELM <3 — Extreme light pollution conditions. The night sky is bright enough that only the Moon, planets, and a few of the brightest stars can be seen. The Milky Way is completely invisible, even on the clearest nights.
